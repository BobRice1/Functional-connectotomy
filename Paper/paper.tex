\begin{abstract}
We reproduced and extended the Hopf whole-brain modelling framework of Alexandersen et al.~\cite{Alexandersen2025} for glioma-related changes in phase-lag index (PLI) functional connectivity. The model fits empirical PLI by varying global coupling strength $K$ and local excitability $\lambda$, with fits organised primarily by the normalised coupling $\kappa = K/\sqrt{\lambda}$. We qualitatively reproduced the original findings that glioma requires higher effective coupling than control, and that removing tumour-related regions from the PLI comparison reduces fitted coupling. We then extended the analysis in two ways: first, by applying virtual lesioning systematically to all 78 AAL regions, and second, by directly weakening structural connections of selected functional driver regions. Virtual lesioning revealed spatially heterogeneous coupling effects, with glioma-specific effects concentrated mainly in frontal and medial regions and associated with eigenvector centrality. However, structural perturbation of top virtual-lesioning drivers did not move control fits toward the higher-coupling glioma regime. These results support the interpretation that glioma alters the modelled balance between local oscillatory dynamics and interregional coupling, while showing that functional connectotomy should not be interpreted as anatomical disconnection.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Understanding how large-scale brain activity emerges from interactions between distributed neural populations is a central problem in computational neuroscience. Whole-brain models address this by combining local neural dynamics with large-scale structural connectivity, allowing functional connectivity patterns to be reproduced and interpreted mechanistically \cite{Deco2011,Deco2014}. A common approach is to represent brain regions as coupled oscillators, such that synchronisation between regions gives rise to coherent large-scale activity \cite{Cabral2011}. Among these models, Hopf oscillators are particularly useful because they capture the transition between stable and oscillatory dynamics while remaining mathematically tractable \cite{Freyer2011}.

Alexandersen et al.~\cite{Alexandersen2025} used a Hopf whole-brain model to investigate glioma-related alterations in brain dynamics. Fitting the model to phase-lag index (PLI) functional connectivity, they showed that the phase dynamics are governed primarily by a normalised coupling parameter reflecting the balance between interregional coupling and local excitability, rather than by either quantity independently. They further reported that this balance is shifted in glioma patients toward stronger effective global interactions, and that tumour-related regions contribute to this shift in connectotomy analyses. These results suggest that glioma alters not only local neural activity, but also the relative contribution of large-scale network interactions to whole-brain dynamics.

In this report, \emph{fitted coupling} refers to the value of the model's global coupling parameter $K$ that gives the best match between simulated and empirical PLI matrices. Because the model fit is governed by $\kappa = K/\sqrt{\lambda}$, fitted coupling should be interpreted as an effective model-level measure of interregional influence relative to local oscillatory amplitude, not as a direct measurement of anatomical white-matter strength. We also distinguish two forms of lesion analysis. \emph{Functional connectotomy} or \emph{virtual lesioning} removes a region from the PLI comparison only: the region remains present in the simulation, but its row and column are excluded when correlating simulated and empirical connectivity. \emph{Structural perturbation} instead changes the simulated network itself by weakening the anatomical connections of a selected region before running the model.

In this project, we first reproduce the main dynamical findings of Alexandersen et al.~\cite{Alexandersen2025}: the approximate $K/\sqrt{\lambda}$ scaling relation, the shift toward higher fitted coupling in glioma, and the reduction in fitted coupling after tumour-region connectotomy. We then extend the original connectotomy analysis in two targeted ways. The original paper focused on tumour-related region sets; our first extension applies the same masking logic systematically to every AAL region, producing a whole-brain atlas of region-wise coupling sensitivity. Our second extension asks whether these functional sensitivity results correspond to anatomical vulnerability by directly weakening the structural connections of selected functional driver regions before simulation. Together, these analyses distinguish regions that influence the fitting objective from regions whose structural disconnection is sufficient to reproduce glioma-like model behaviour.

\section{Methods}
\subsection{Reproduction of the original model}
The reproduction was implemented by first validating the local and pairwise Hopf dynamics in reduced settings, and then scaling the model to larger networks and the empirical 78-node connectome used in the original study. This allowed us to verify the numerical implementation before reproducing the whole-brain fitting, scaling-law, and connectotomy analyses.

\subsection{Hopf oscillator model}
At each node, the dynamics were governed by the Hopf normal form
\begin{equation}
\dot{z}_i = z_i\left(\lambda + i\omega_i - |z_i|^2\right)
+ K \tanh\!\left(C \sum_{j=1}^{N} w_{ij} x_j\right),
\end{equation}
where $z_i = x_i + i y_i$ is the complex state of node $i$, $\lambda$ is the excitability parameter, $\omega_i$ is the intrinsic angular frequency, $K$ is the global coupling strength, $C$ is a structural scaling constant, and $w_{ij}$ are the entries of the structural connectivity matrix. As in the original paper, the real part $x_i$ was treated as the simulated neural signal.

\subsection{Reduced-model validation}
Before reproducing the empirical whole-brain results, we verified the Hopf model in simplified settings to confirm that the implementation captured the expected local and pairwise dynamics. First, we analysed a single uncoupled Hopf oscillator by setting $N=1$ and $K=0$, and simulated the system across subcritical, critical, and supercritical values of $\lambda$. This recovered the standard Hopf bifurcation structure, with trajectories decaying to the origin for $\lambda<0$ and converging to stable oscillations of amplitude $\sqrt{\lambda}$ for $\lambda>0$. We then considered a two-node system with symmetric coupling to examine the onset of phase locking and the dependence of phase-based synchronisation on coupling strength and excitability. For a range of $K$, $\lambda$, and intrinsic frequency differences, we computed the phase lag index (PLI) from the filtered real-valued signals and confirmed that synchronisation increases with coupling and is approximately organised by the normalised coupling
\begin{equation}
\kappa = \frac{K}{\sqrt{\lambda}}.
\end{equation}
These reduced-model tests served primarily as validation and provided intuition for the scaling behaviour investigated later in the empirical network model.

\subsection{Network simulations}
The final stage of the reproduction scaled the model to larger networks. Before fitting empirical data, synthetic network tests were used to confirm that the model produced plausible oscillatory dynamics and structured PLI matrices.
The empirical-scale analysis then used a 78-node structural connectivity matrix together with cortical frequency values provided in the empirical dataset used for the reproduction. The empirical inputs consisted of the cohort-averaged control and glioma PLI matrices, a 78-region AAL structural connectivity matrix, and region-wise peak-frequency values derived from the accompanying data of the original study. Control and glioma PLI matrices were used as empirical fitting targets. Simulations matched the original signal-processing pipeline as closely as possible: the real-valued node signals were simulated, filtered in the alpha band, transformed into phases using the Hilbert transform, and converted into pairwise PLI matrices. For each pair of regions, PLI was computed as the absolute mean sign of the phase difference over time, so that values near zero indicate symmetric phase differences and values near one indicate a consistent non-zero phase lag~\cite{Stam2007}. 

\subsection{Parameter fitting and comparison with empirical data}
To reproduce the fitting procedure in the original paper, a two-dimensional grid search over coupling strength $K$ and excitability $\lambda$ was performed. For each parameter pair, the simulated PLI matrix was compared with the empirical PLI using the Pearson correlation between the upper-triangular entries of the matrices. Repeated simulations over multiple random initial conditions were used to obtain more stable estimates of fit quality. 
This fitting procedure was first used to reproduce the control-cohort parameter landscape corresponding to Figure~2 of the original paper. A shuffled-connectivity null model was then introduced by randomising the empirical structural connectivity matrix while preserving the weight distribution, in order to test whether the observed fit depended on the true network topology. Thresholded comparisons were also carried out using top-3\% PLI matrices, matching the thresholding procedure implemented in the network simulations. 

\subsection{Scaling-law reproduction}
The log--log parameter search described in the paper was reproduced by evaluating the goodness of fit over logarithmically spaced grids of $K$ and $\lambda$. The resulting fit surface was examined along lines of constant
\begin{equation}
K = \kappa \sqrt{\lambda},
\end{equation}
to test the paper's claim that the phase dynamics are approximately invariant when the normalised coupling $\kappa$ is held fixed. This formed the central theoretical reproduction of the scaling result.

\subsection{Glioma fitting and connectotomy analyses}
The later sections of the reproduction extended the same fitting framework to the glioma cohort. Separate parameter searches were performed against the glioma PLI matrix, and the resulting optimal coupling strengths were compared with the control case across multiple values of $\lambda$. Distributions of optimal coupling differences were then computed over repeated initial conditions, together with Wilcoxon tests, to reproduce the cohort-level comparisons in the original study. 
Finally, functional connectotomy analyses were implemented to reproduce the paper's Figures~6--8. Tumour-related regions were removed from the PLI comparison stage, the model was refit, and the resulting changes in optimal coupling were analysed for tumour-region groupings and pooled distributions. This allowed the contribution of tumour regions to elevated coupling strength to be assessed within the reproduced modelling framework.

\subsection{Virtual lesioning atlas}
To extend the connectotomy analysis beyond the predefined tumour-region sets used in Alexandersen et al. ~\cite{Alexandersen2025}, a systematic virtual-lesioning analysis was performed over all 78 AAL regions, based on the automated anatomical labelling parcellation~\cite{TzourioMazoyer2002}. The same spectral-normalised empirical structural connectivity matrix, raw control and glioma PLI matrices, and subject-mean cortical frequencies used in the reproduction were retained. Simulations were conducted at a fixed excitability value $\lambda = 40$, corresponding to the best-fit empirical value identified in the reproduction.
For each of 300 random initial conditions, the global coupling parameter $K$ was swept over 60 linearly spaced values between $0.5$ and $50$. At each $(K,\lambda)$ pair and for each initial condition, the whole-brain Hopf model was simulated once using structural scaling constant $C=20$, total simulated time $t_{\mathrm{total}}=8.0$, discarded transient time $t_{\mathrm{discard}}=1.5$, and sampling frequency $f_s = 300$, yielding a single simulated PLI matrix. This matrix was compared with the empirical control and glioma PLI matrices using the Pearson correlation between the upper-triangular matrix entries. To implement virtual lesioning, each region was removed in turn from the comparison stage by deleting the corresponding row and column from both the simulated and empirical PLI matrices, after which the correlation was recomputed on the reduced matrices. 
For each initial condition and each region $i$, the parameter sweep yielded an optimal full-network coupling $K_{\mathrm{full}}^{*}$ and a region-masked optimum $K_{\mathrm{lesion},i}^{*}$, defined as the values of $K$ that maximised the correlation with the empirical data. The lesioning effect of region $i$ was then quantified as
\begin{equation}
\Delta K_i = K_{\mathrm{lesion},i}^{*} - K_{\mathrm{full}}^{*},
\end{equation}
computed separately for the control and glioma comparisons. Negative values of $\Delta K_i$ indicate that masking a region reduces the fitted optimal coupling, implying that the region contributes positively to the elevated coupling required to reproduce the empirical functional connectivity. 

To isolate disease-specific effects, a differential lesioning score was defined as
\begin{equation}
\Delta K_i^{\mathrm{diff}} = \Delta K_i^{\mathrm{glioma}} - \Delta K_i^{\mathrm{control}}.
\end{equation}
This quantity captures the extent to which a region's contribution to coupling strength is specific to the glioma cohort, rather than being a general feature of the model fit. 

Per-region effects were summarised across initial conditions by their mean and the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles. Statistical evidence for non-zero lesioning effects was assessed using Wilcoxon signed-rank tests across initial conditions, applied to the non-zero entries of each regional effect vector, with significance evaluated using a Bonferroni-corrected threshold over the 78 regional tests. Because these tests were performed across random initial conditions, they quantify robustness to simulation variability rather than subject-level population inference. The empirical PLI targets were cohort averages, so these statistics should not be interpreted as tests of between-subject variability.

To assess whether strong functional coupling drivers coincide with structurally important regions, three graph-theoretic measures were computed from the same spectral-normalised empirical structural connectivity matrix for each region: degree, weighted strength, and eigenvector centrality. Spearman rank correlations were used to compare these measures with the lesioning effect sizes, separately for control and glioma fits. Finally, enrichment of the top-ranked lesioning drivers within the tumour-region set used in Alexandersen et al. ~\cite{Alexandersen2025} was assessed using a hypergeometric overlap test for top-$N$ sets with $N \in \{5,10,15,20,30\}$. For the overlap analysis, the tumour set was represented as 19 regions grouped into Frontal L, Temporal L, and Frontal R categories.

Together, these analyses produced a region-wise atlas of functional coupling drivers and tested whether the connectotomy effect can be explained by the structural importance of the affected regions.

\subsection{Structural connectivity perturbation}
As a second extension, we tested whether anatomically damaging regions identified by the virtual-lesioning atlas changes the fitted global coupling in the whole-brain Hopf model. In contrast to the virtual-lesioning analysis, which removed regions only from the PLI comparison stage, this analysis directly perturbed the structural connectivity matrix used in the simulations and then refit the model.

The empirical structural connectivity matrix was loaded and spectral-normalised to unit spectral radius, matching the normalisation used in the virtual-lesioning atlas. Raw empirical PLI matrices for both the control and glioma cohorts were used as fitting targets, and subject-mean cortical frequencies were loaded from the empirical frequency file and assigned region-wise in the simulations. To keep the perturbation analysis in the same parameter regime as the virtual-lesioning atlas, the same fixed excitability value ($\lambda = 40$) and the same 60-point coupling grid ($K \in [0.5, 50]$) were reused from the virtual-lesioning analysis results.

Regions for perturbation were selected from the control virtual-lesioning ranking. The 10 regions with the most negative mean control lesioning scores $\Delta K$ were defined operationally as \emph{functional drivers}, because masking them in the virtual-lesioning analysis produced the largest downward shifts in fitted coupling. This term refers only to influence on the fitting objective and does not imply that these regions are causal biological drivers of glioma pathology. As a null comparison set, 5 \emph{non-driver} regions were also selected as those with the smallest absolute control lesioning effects. Idealised structural damage was implemented as a graded dose-response manipulation. For a chosen region and damage level $d \in \{0,0.25,0.5,0.75,1.0\}$, all incoming and outgoing structural connections of that region were scaled by $1-d$, that is, both the corresponding row and column of the structural connectivity matrix were multiplied by this factor. Thus, $d=0$ left the connectome unchanged, whereas $d=1$ fully disconnected the perturbed node. 
This tested whether functional coupling drivers also behaved as anatomical damage drivers: if so, weakening them in the control connectome should shift the control fit toward the higher-coupling glioma regime.
For each region, damage level, and initial condition, the Hopf model was simulated across the full reused $K$ grid while keeping $\lambda$ fixed. Simulations used the same settings as in the virtual-lesioning atlas: total simulation time $t_{\mathrm{total}}=8.0$, discarded transient time $t_{\mathrm{discard}}=1.5$, and sampling frequency $f_s=300$. To improve robustness, 50 initial conditions were sampled for each region-damage combination, with deterministic seeds indexed by region, damage level, and initial-condition number. For each simulated PLI matrix, model fit was quantified as the Pearson correlation between the upper-triangular entries of the simulated matrix and the corresponding empirical control or glioma PLI matrix. The best-fitting coupling values $K^{*}_{\mathrm{control}}$ and $K^{*}_{\mathrm{glioma}}$ were then defined as the coupling values that maximised these correlations for the two cohorts separately.

Across initial conditions, mean and standard deviation of the fitted $K^{*}$ values were retained for every region and damage level. Dose-response sensitivity was summarised by fitting linear slopes of $K^{*}$ versus damage fraction, using both the full damage range and a primary mild-damage range ($d \leq 0.5$) to avoid the full-disconnection case dominating the estimate. These summaries were used to test whether structural damage to top functional coupling-driver regions produced steeper coupling shifts than damage to non-driver regions, and whether damaging driver regions pushed the control fit toward the higher-coupling regime observed in glioma.





\section{Results}

\subsection{Reduced-model validation}
Before turning to the empirical whole-brain model, we verified that the numerical implementation reproduced the expected Hopf dynamics in reduced settings. For a single uncoupled oscillator, simulations recovered the canonical Hopf bifurcation: trajectories decayed to the origin for $\lambda<0$, remained at the bifurcation boundary for $\lambda=0$, and converged to stable limit-cycle oscillations for $\lambda>0$. The steady-state amplitude followed the theoretical prediction $|z|=\sqrt{\lambda}$, confirming that the implementation correctly captures the dependence of local oscillatory amplitude on excitability. In a symmetric two-node system, increasing coupling strength promoted phase locking and increased the phase lag index (PLI), with synchronisation approximately organised by the normalised coupling
\begin{equation}
\kappa = \frac{K}{\sqrt{\lambda}}.
\end{equation}
Taken together, these reduced-model results served mainly as validation and provided intuition for the scaling structure examined in the empirical network model.

\subsection{Empirical network fitting and scaling law}
We next scaled the model to the empirical 78-node structural connectome and compared simulated phase-lag connectivity with the empirical control-cohort PLI matrix. The simulated PLI reproduced broad block-structured organisation also visible in the empirical data, indicating that the coupled Hopf network captures non-trivial large-scale structure rather than producing homogeneous synchrony (Fig.~\ref{fig:network_matrices}).

To quantify model performance, we performed a grid search over coupling strength $K$ and excitability $\lambda$, using the Pearson correlation between simulated and empirical PLI as the goodness-of-fit measure. The best fits occurred for positive values of $\lambda$, and the high-fit region extended diagonally through parameter space (Fig.~\ref{fig:network_scaling}). When the same search was plotted on log--log axes, the fit remained approximately constant along lines of the form
\begin{equation}
K = \kappa \sqrt{\lambda},
\end{equation}
showing that the phase-based functional connectivity is governed primarily by the balance between interregional coupling and local excitability rather than by $K$ and $\lambda$ independently. This qualitatively reproduces the central scaling-law result of the original study.

\subsection{Glioma-control comparison}
We then compared the fitted parameter landscapes for the control and glioma cohort averages. Across excitability values, the glioma cohort was shifted toward higher optimal coupling strengths than the control cohort (Fig.~\ref{fig:network_glioma}). This difference was also reflected in the fitted normalised-coupling lines, which gave a larger effective $\kappa$ for glioma than for control. The fitted normalised coupling was \(\kappa_{\mathrm{control}} = 5.460\) for the control cohort and \(\kappa_{\mathrm{glioma}} = 6.046\) for the glioma cohort, corresponding to an approximately \(10.7\%\) increase in the glioma fit. Thus, the reproduced whole-brain model supports the original paper's central claim that glioma-related dynamics are associated with stronger interregional interactions relative to local oscillatory dynamics.

\subsection{Functional connectotomy}
Finally, we reproduced the functional connectotomy analysis by removing tumour-related regions from the PLI comparison stage and refitting the model. In the glioma fit, masking tumour-region sets generally reduced the absolute fitted coupling, consistent with the direction of the original connectotomy effect. However, the control-relative change was heterogeneous across tumour sets: some tumour masks produced larger reductions in the control comparison than in the glioma comparison. We therefore interpret this as a qualitative reproduction of the connectotomy direction rather than an exact patient-level replication of the original effect.

\subsection{Virtual lesioning atlas}
To extend the connectotomy analysis beyond the predefined tumour-region sets used in the original paper, we evaluated the lesioning effect of all 78 AAL regions on the fitted optimal coupling. The resulting region-wise atlas showed that lesioning effects were spatially heterogeneous rather than uniform across the brain (Fig.~\ref{fig:ranked_dk}). In the glioma fit, a subset of regions produced noticeably larger changes in the optimal coupling than the rest, whereas the control profile was comparatively flatter when shown in the same regional order. This suggests that the elevated coupling required to reproduce the glioma functional connectivity is driven disproportionately by specific regions rather than being distributed evenly across the network.
To isolate disease-specific effects, we compared the lesioning responses in glioma and control using the differential score
\[
\Delta K_i^{\mathrm{diff}}=\Delta K_i^{\mathrm{glioma}}-\Delta K_i^{\mathrm{control}}.
\]
The resulting differential atlas identified a subset of regions whose masking reduced the fitted coupling more strongly in glioma than in control (Fig.~\ref{fig:differential_atlas}). The strongest positive differential effects were concentrated mainly in frontal and medial regions, including bilateral medial orbital and superior medial frontal areas, together with posterior cingulate and a smaller number of occipital and parietal regions. In contrast, several sensorimotor, insular, and subcortical regions showed negative differential effects, indicating weaker or more control-like lesioning responses. Taken together, these results move beyond the original grouped tumour-region connectotomy analysis by producing a systematic all-region sensitivity map of candidate functional coupling drivers.

\subsection{Relation to structural connectivity}
We next asked whether the strongest virtual-lesioning effects could be explained by the structural importance of the affected regions. Degree and weighted strength were not significantly associated with lesioning effect size in either cohort (control: degree $\rho=0.07$, $p=5.28\times10^{-1}$; strength $\rho=-0.04$, $p=7.36\times10^{-1}$; glioma: degree $\rho=0.01$, $p=9.55\times10^{-1}$; strength $\rho=-0.05$, $p=6.81\times10^{-1}$; Fig.~\ref{fig:hubness_vs_dk}). By contrast, eigenvector centrality showed a significant positive association with lesioning effect size in both cohorts, with a stronger relationship in glioma than in control (control: $\rho=0.32$, $p=4.51\times10^{-3}$; glioma: $\rho=0.41$, $p=1.94\times10^{-4}$; Fig.~\ref{fig:hubness_vs_dk}). Thus, in this analysis, the strongest functional coupling drivers are not simply the nodes with the largest number or weight of connections, but are more closely related to globally influential regions in the structural network.
Overall, the extension suggests that the connectotomy effect reported in the original paper is not only recoverable at the level of grouped tumour regions, but can be resolved into a whole-brain atlas of region-specific coupling drivers, with the clearest glioma-specific effects emerging in a subset of structurally influential frontal and medial regions.
The overlap analysis showed that the top-ranked differential virtual-lesioning drivers were enriched for the tumour-region set used in the original paper at small \(N\): the top-5 differential drivers contained 4 tumour-set regions (\(p = 0.011\), hypergeometric test). However, this enrichment weakened for larger top-\(N\) sets, indicating that the overlap is concentrated among the strongest glioma-specific drivers rather than being a uniform property of the full ranking.

\subsection{Structural connectivity perturbation}
We next tested whether directly damaging the structural connections of the control coupling-driver regions identified by the virtual-lesioning atlas would alter the fitted coupling regime. The top 10 control drivers included left posterior cingulate cortex, bilateral medial orbitofrontal cortex, left superior medial frontal cortex, and left inferior orbitofrontal cortex, and were contrasted with 5 non-driver regions whose lesioning effects were close to zero.

At baseline ($d=0$), the fitted coupling was consistently higher for the glioma target than for the control target across all tested regions, reproducing the cohort shift seen in the main analysis. For the driver set, mean fitted values were typically $K^{*}_{\mathrm{control}} \approx 34$--$35$ and $K^{*}_{\mathrm{glioma}} \approx 36$--$37$, with similar baseline values in the non-driver set. The full dose-response curves are shown in Fig.~\ref{fig:scpert_dose_response}.

Progressive structural damage did change the fitted coupling, but not in the hypothesised driver-specific direction. Across the mild damage range ($0 \leq d \leq 0.5$), the slope of $K^{*}_{\mathrm{control}}$ versus damage had mean $-0.752$ for driver regions and mean $-1.946$ for non-driver regions. Over the full range ($0 \leq d \leq 1$), the corresponding mean slopes were $-2.583$ for drivers and $-2.497$ for non-drivers. Thus, damaging the top coupling drivers did not produce larger or steeper coupling shifts than damaging the null regions; if anything, the mild-range effect was numerically stronger in the non-driver set. The perturbation analysis therefore did not support the prediction that virtual-lesioning analysis driver status would translate directly into heightened structural sensitivity in the structural-perturbation analysis (Fig.~\ref{fig:scpert_sensitivity}).

The perturbation also failed to push the control fit toward the glioma-like coupling regime. Averaged across driver regions, the control-glioma gap in fitted coupling was $\langle K^{*}_{\mathrm{control}} \rangle - \langle K^{*}_{\mathrm{glioma}} \rangle = -1.629$ at baseline, $-1.770$ at $d=0.5$, and $-3.584$ at full damage. The corresponding non-driver gap changed from $-1.339$ at baseline to $-1.832$ at $d=0.5$ and $-2.883$ at full damage. Rather than converging toward the glioma fit, the control fit therefore moved further away from it as damage increased, especially under extreme ablation (Fig.~\ref{fig:scpert_convergence}).

Taken together, these results indicate that direct structural damage alone was not sufficient to reproduce the elevated-coupling signature associated with glioma. The perturbation manipulation altered fitted coupling, but it did not selectively amplify the effect of the strongest functional drivers and did not generate control-to-glioma convergence. Within this implementation, the glioma shift therefore cannot be explained as a simple consequence of weakening the structural connectivity of the virtual-lesioning analysis driver regions.

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Reproduction of the original model}

This project qualitatively reproduced the central dynamical findings of Alexandersen et al.~\cite{Alexandersen2025} and extended their connectotomy framework using region-wise virtual lesioning and structural-connectivity perturbation. At the reproduction level, the Hopf model recovered the main structure of the original result: fits were organised primarily by the normalised coupling $\kappa=K/\sqrt{\lambda}$, glioma required higher effective coupling than control, and tumour-region connectotomy reduced fitted coupling in the expected direction. These results should be interpreted as a qualitative reproduction rather than an exact numerical replication, since fitted values can depend on implementation choices, simulation duration, thresholding, grid resolution, and random initial conditions.

\subsection{Interpretation of fitted coupling}

The fitted coupling parameter should not be interpreted as a direct biological measure of anatomical connectivity. Because the model fit is organised by $\kappa=K/\sqrt{\lambda}$, a higher fitted $K$ is meaningful mainly relative to the local oscillatory amplitude controlled by $\lambda$. The glioma-control difference is therefore better described as a shift in effective dynamical balance: the model requires stronger interregional influence relative to local dynamics to reproduce the glioma PLI structure.

\subsection{Region-wise virtual lesioning}

The virtual-lesioning extension showed that fitted coupling sensitivity is spatially heterogeneous. By masking each of the 78 AAL regions from the PLI comparison, we obtained a whole-brain atlas of regions that influence the fitting objective. The strongest glioma-specific effects were concentrated mainly in frontal and medial regions, and the small-$N$ tumour-overlap result suggests that the strongest differential drivers partly coincide with the tumour-region set used in the original paper. The association with eigenvector centrality, but not degree or weighted strength, further suggests that these effects depend more on global structural network position than on simple connection count or total connection weight, consistent with graph-theoretic approaches to structural and functional brain networks~\cite{BullmoreSporns2009}.

\subsection{Structural perturbation}

The structural-connectivity perturbation analysis provided an important qualification. Unlike virtual lesioning, which removes a region only from the PLI comparison, structural perturbation directly weakens that region's anatomical connections before simulation. If virtual-lesioning drivers were also anatomical damage drivers, weakening them should have moved control fits toward the higher-coupling glioma regime. This was not observed: driver regions did not show stronger damage sensitivity than non-drivers, and structural damage generally increased rather than reduced the control--glioma coupling gap.

\subsection{Overall interpretation}

Together, the extensions show that functional connectotomy is best understood as a sensitivity analysis of the fitting objective, not as a direct simulation of tumour-induced anatomical disconnection. The virtual-lesioning atlas identifies regions that influence the empirical--simulated PLI comparison, whereas the perturbation analysis tests whether anatomical weakening changes the simulated network in a glioma-like direction, motivated by evidence that resting-state functional connectivity is constrained by underlying structural connectivity~\cite{Honey2009,Cabral2012}. The results therefore support the original interpretation that glioma alters the modelled balance between local oscillatory dynamics and interregional coupling, while showing that functional lesioning drivers do not necessarily behave as structural damage drivers.

\section{Limitations}
Several limitations should be considered when interpreting these results. First, statistical tests performed across random initial conditions quantify simulation robustness rather than subject-level population inference, since the empirical targets were cohort-averaged PLI matrices. Second, the Hopf whole-brain model is a simplified phenomenological description of neuronal dynamics, so fitted parameters should be interpreted as model-level descriptors rather than direct physiological quantities. Third, both the reproduction and the extension are based on static, phase-based functional connectivity derived from PLI, and therefore do not capture temporal fluctuations or more complex metastable dynamics~\cite{Cabral2011}. Fourth, the framework inherits simplifying assumptions from the original study, including the use of a fixed structural connectome, thresholded PLI matrices, and predominantly global parameters, which may reduce sensitivity to subject-specific or region-specific mechanisms. Finally, the virtual-lesioning atlas is a model-based measure of how strongly masking a region changes fitted coupling, and should not be interpreted as a direct causal map of tumour influence in vivo. The same caution applies to the structural-perturbation extension: scaling all incoming and outgoing edges of a node is only one idealised way to model anatomical damage, and the negative result does not rule out more selective or biologically grounded perturbations producing different outcomes.

\section{Future directions}
Future work should test more biologically specific perturbation schemes rather than uniformly scaling all incoming and outgoing connections of a region. For example, perturbations could target tumour-adjacent edges, long-range fibres, or connections weighted by empirical lesion maps. The model could also be extended beyond global parameters by allowing region-specific changes in coupling or excitability. Finally, applying the framework to subject-level PLI and structural connectivity would allow testing whether region-wise lesioning effects predict individual tumour location, symptom profile, or clinical outcome.

\section*{Conclusion}
We reproduced the main qualitative findings of Alexandersen et al., including the \(K/\sqrt{\lambda}\) scaling relation, higher fitted coupling in glioma, and reduced coupling after functional connectotomy. Our extensions showed that coupling effects are regionally heterogeneous and partly associated with eigenvector centrality, but that functional lesioning drivers do not translate directly into structural damage drivers. The project therefore supports the interpretation that glioma shifts the balance between local and interregional dynamics, while showing that connectotomy effects are best interpreted as sensitivity of the functional fitting objective rather than direct anatomical disconnection.
